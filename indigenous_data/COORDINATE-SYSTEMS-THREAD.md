# Coordinate Systems Thread — Research & Design Notes

## The Core Insight

A student who learns the coordinate plane through weaving understands something that a student who learns it abstractly often doesn't: **the axes are a reference frame, not data points.**

In weaving, the warp threads (vertical, fixed) are the loom's structure. They don't move. The weft threads (horizontal, woven) are positioned *against* the warp. Nobody confuses the loom frame with a thread — the distinction is physically obvious.

This directly addresses one of the most persistent K-5 coordinate plane misconceptions: **confusing the x-axis with x = 1, or confusing an axis label with a coordinate value.** If a child has already experienced a physical reference frame (the loom), the abstract notation makes immediate sense.

## The K-12 Vertical Thread

This thread traces one conceptual idea — "how do we describe position using a reference system?" — from kindergarten through trigonometry. Each grade band builds on the previous one.

### K-2: Physical Reference Frames
- **Weaving a Grid (K2-08):** Paper strip weaving on a loom card. Warp = frame that stays still. Weft = what moves. "Can you describe any position using two numbers?" This is the concrete phase — bodies and hands.
- **Four Directions Orientation (K2-09):** Stand in the room, face North. Walk 3 steps North, 2 steps East. The child IS the origin. The cardinal directions ARE the axes. Many Indigenous nations use four-directions as a fundamental organizing framework (✜ in MN standards).

### 3-5: Coordinate Notation
- **Bead Loom Coordinates (35-09):** Eglash's Virtual Bead Loom at csdt.org. Students TYPE (x, y) to place beads — they can't click. This forces the distinction: the grid is the reference, the bead is the point. Four-fold symmetry connects to four-directions worldview = four quadrants. RCT evidence of math gains.
- **Weaving Patterns as Coordinate Plots (35-10):** Bridge from physical weaving to abstract notation. "Write the coordinates of every red square in this woven pattern." Students discover a design IS a set of coordinate pairs — same information, different representation.

### 6-8: Alternative Coordinate Systems
- **Star Navigation Angles (68-09):** Lipka's MCC module. Yup'ik navigators use star elevation and landmark angles. Students learn angular measurement through navigation, not protractor drills. IES-funded RCT evidence.
- **D/Lakota and Ojibwe Star Maps (68-10):** Annette Lee's Native Skywatchers. Star maps with Polaris at center = polar coordinates. This is the first time students encounter the idea that **there's more than one way to describe position.** A grid isn't the only option. When is circular better than rectangular?
- **Indigenous Cartography (68-11):** Compare Western grid maps with Indigenous relational maps. Critical thinking: what does each system capture? What does it miss? WHY do we choose different coordinate systems?

### 9-12: Trigonometry & Formal Systems
- **Polynesian Wayfinding and Trigonometry (912-08):** Pacific navigators divided the horizon into 32 houses of 11.25° each. They determined latitude from star elevation using what we now call trigonometric ratios. They mentally performed vector addition for wind and current correction. This is polar coordinates and trig, practiced for centuries before European formalization.
- **Coordinate System Comparison (912-09):** Capstone. Convert star positions from polar (r, θ) to Cartesian (x, y). When is each system more useful? The IK-HABME insight: all coordinate systems are human inventions. Different cultures invented different ones for different purposes. None is "more correct."

## What's Still Needed

1. **Classroom pilot testing** — These entries are research-backed but haven't been tested as a connected K-12 sequence. A pilot with even one grade band would generate valuable feedback.
2. **Indigenous educator review** — Every entry should be reviewed by educators from the relevant nations before classroom use. The engine flags this in its disclaimer.
3. **More Ojibwe/Dakota-specific content** — The MN standards specifically require Dakota and Anishinaabe integration. The star maps are perfectly aligned; the Polynesian wayfinding is supplementary (important math, different cultural context).
4. **Student misconception mapping** — Your x-axis insight is one example. A systematic mapping of which misconceptions each entry addresses would make the engine even more useful for the Digester.
